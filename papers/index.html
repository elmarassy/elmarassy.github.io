The Quantum Path Integral and its applications to Statistical Mechanics

This was my final paper for 8.06 (quantum III) at MIT. At the time I was also taking 18.676 (stochastic calculus), and I was interested in learning more about how it could be applied to physics.

The paper discusses how stochastic processes like Brownian motion can be used to construct path integral solutions to PDEs including the Schrodinger equation. It then applies the quantum path integral to solve statistical mechanics problems. I really enjoyed researching this paper, and I got to learn some interesting interpretations of the physics I had studied in class.

8.13 Experimental Physics Papers

I wrote these papers as part of my experimental physics class (8.13) in Spring 2025. I learned a lot about data analysis while doing these experiments. I also had a lot of fun with thinking of creative new techniques for some of them where the analysis was more open ended.

CosmicWatch: Measuring the attenuation of cosmic ray muons in water

This was the last paper of the year for 8.13. We were given some cosmic ray muon detectors called CosmicWatches, and asked to design our own open ended experiment that made use of them.

I measured the attenuation of muons in water, which is the rate of energy loss as they travel through a medium. This determines a quantity called the meter water equivalence, which is used by particle physics experiments to determine how much shielding they achieve from background radiation.

I made muon rate measurements at the Charles River (at the docks and on a motorboat) and at White Pond in Concord, MA (in a canoe)! I used the rates and depths to determine the attenuation, and I had fun applying different corrections to account for the angular acceptance of the detectors and fluctuations due to atmospheric pressure.

Optical Trapping: Measuring Boltzmann's constant through the Brownian motion of beads

This was the second major 8.13 experiment. I did the optical trapping experiment, in which we use a laser to create a potential well in which we can analyze and influence the motion of microscopic particles.

We began by measuring the motion of different sizes of beads under different laser powers, which changes the strength of the trap. Using these data, we were able to get a measurement of Boltzmann's constant from the power spectral density of the oscillations.

I made another independent measurement of the constant that used the amplitude of the bead oscillations, which I was able to obtain after calibrating the trap positions. Finally, I made a third measurement using Stokes' drag force, which we were able to do by knowing the diameter of the beads.

This information was used to calibrate the trap for measuring velocities and microscopic forces in cell organelles.

Relativistic Dynamics: Energy-Momentum relations for relativistic electrons

My first 8.13 experiment was relativistic dynamics, where we determine the energy, momentum, and velocity relations of relativistic electrons.

We made measurements of these three quantities, and performed fits to classical and relativistic predictions to demonstrate that Newtonian mechanics does not describe fast electrons.

As a bonus, we were able to determine the electron charge to mass ratio as part of our analysis.

This experiment was very interesting, but unfortunately our radioactive source wasn't aligned properly. This caused us to get very low count rates (~10x less), which made the data taking process take many extra sessions :(

Geometric Brownian motion with the Fokker Planck equation

This was numerical project for 8.08 (statistical mechanics II) where we studied non-equilibrium systems.

I decided to generalize a process we studied in class (geometric Brownian motion) and apply it to some real life examples. I then did some basic simulations and compared the results with my predictions from the Fokker-Planck equation.

This was a fun, small project where I learned about simulating random processes. I ended up using some of the concepts I learned here to write my 8.06 quantum path integral project.

Woodworking

I have been woodworking/building things in the garage for 7 years. Here are some of my favorite creations

Remote control F-14 Tomcat scale model

December 2022 - April 2023

This was my largest and most difficult project, but it was also my favorite by far. There were so many interesting challenges that came up, and I had fun trying to solve them all. I was super happy with the final result, especially since the F-14 is my favorite plane.

The process of building the plane took months of planning, trial, and error, but I have documented all of the main steps here.

Here is the completed plane. Built and carved by hand from balsa wood, 49" length, 50" wingspan, retractible landing gear, swing wings, ducted fan motors, FPV camera, and more

Plans, layout, and skeleton

The first step was to plan the project. I found an excellent set of plans from Matt Halton. They were very detailed and gave tips on some of the more challenging parts of the build.

I then bought all of the supplies I would need. I made sure to purchase compatible parts. I bought two 70A motors that would fit within the fuselage, provide lots of thrust, and not require too large of a battery. I bought a lot of balsa wood to build the body of the plane, and dozens of random parts that I needed for different systems.

The right image above shows my initial setup. I used my workbench and desk (which was a work in progress at the time) as work surfaces. I printed out full scale plans to use as templates, and a smaller 0.5x scale version that I used for reference.

I my templates to cut out the main structural pieces for the fuselage. I made these pieces out of a special type of extra light plywood since they needed to be stronger than the balsa wood used for the other parts. I cut them out on my bandsaw and ended up with the pieces shown on the bottom right.

Reinforcing with fiberglass, initial fuselage assembly

As seen in the images, the skeletal pieces of the fuselage were extremely thin. To make sure they would not break under the weight of a large plane, I reinforced them with some fiberglass. I purchased a small roll and applied it using some epoxy I had from my woodworking projects.

I then assembled the front of the fuselage. The bottom was a thick piece of balsa wood that I shaped with a knife to match the profile of the plane. I then smoothed it out with some sandpaper.

I then assembled the back half of the fuselage, which is there the ducts for the motors was. The skeleton for this half was very difficult, because it was extremely thin in some areas to allow clearance for the intake ducts. I also reinforced this section with fiberglass.

At this stage, I also installed the two EDF (electronic ducted fan) motors. These are in the back of the plane and provide thrust by pulling air through the intake ducts, which are installed in the next step.

Intake ducts

I originally made the intake ducts using a thin, clear plastic that I had in my shop. It was flexible and easy to shape, which made it a nice material for these ducts which needed a tight radius and difficult geometry as seen in the first two images.

Despite being very flexible, the plastic was still super annoying to shape properly and I spent a few hours getting it to work and stay in place. Sadly, as soon as I tested the motors the front intakes collapsed due to the high pressure. I needed a stronger material with reinforcements.

I ended up finding some very thick paper that I had used to make poster boards. I decided to try it, and spent a full day trying to shape it since it was even more difficult than the plastic. I needed to make lots of cuts in areas where the curvature required stretching the material, but I eventually managed to make it work. I reinforced these new ducts with some thin wooden rings that I made on the bandsaw, and it was finally able to withstand the pressure.

I then used balsa wood strips to cover up the fuselage now that I had installed the ducts. I did the same carving process from earlier to shape these strips into a smooth body for the plane.

Wing boxes and wings

One of the most challenging parts of the F-14 is the swing wings. The F-14 is capable of sweeping its wings back, allowing it to reach higher speeds. This requires the addition of a strong hinge, on which the wing can pivot forward and backward. The alignment of the hinges must be perfect so that the angles of the wings match up exactly along the full trajectory of the sweep, or else changing the angle midflight would result in a loss of control.

I made these wing boxes using the same lightweight, strong plywood reinforced with fiberglass that I used for the fuselage skeleton. These needed to be the strongest part of the plane since most of the weight of the plane would be supported by just one point of contact on each side.

I inserted long rods through the hinges in the wing boxes, and tried to align them as well as possible by eye.

I then made a wing design, which took several iterations since the first few weren't strong enough. Once I came up with a suitable version, I built two and temporarily installed them on the hinges.

Next, I dialed in the alignment by carefully measuring the angles along several possible wing sweep configurations, making sure they matched as well as possible. This stage took several hours of work because it was one of the most critical parts of the process.

I then permanently fixed the wing box orientation, removed the wings, covered them with a layer of balsa, and reinstalled them permanently.

Tails, coverings, and carvings

The next step was making the tails. The F-14 has two tails. This model didn't need rudders on the tails, so I simply carved them out of balsa.

I also made the rear elecators, which are controlled by servos at the back of the plane. I installed these servos, which was very difficult due to the tight space in this region (it was already almost fully occupied by the exhaust ducts).

I also carved the nose of the plane out of a solid piece of balsa. I cut out the block on the bandsaw to get a rough shape, and then hand carved the rest with a knife.

Finally, I installed the rest of the electronics at the back of the plane, including the receiver and wiring to the servos/EDF motors. I then covered it up with a sheet of balsa and included a small hatch for access to the electronics. This made it easy to access them for fixing problems later. I also carved the "beaver tail" for the back of the plane.

Landing gear and more carvings

This is a very large plane, and it is relatively heavy compared to most model planes. Therefore it needed good landing gear. I decided to install retractible landing gear, which would allow me to use large enough landing gear while not impacting the flying too much.

It was hard to fit the landing gear due to all the other stuff in the plane, like the batteries and intake, but I eventually found a configuration that worked.

I then made a shell to cover the wing sweep area. This was a challenging region to cover because the wings needed to be mobile.

Finally, I carved the exhaust ports for the back of the plane, and covered the remaining gaps along the rest of the body.

As shown in the images above, the plane was now capable of fully retracting and extending the wings! This was my favorite feature.

Making the clear canopy

Lastly, I worked on making the clear canopy for the cockpit. I started by carving the shape I wanted out of a leftover balsa block. Originally my plan was to just color it blue and act like it was glass, but I didn't like how it looked.

I decided I wanted to have a clear canopy, but I wasn't able to find a way to buy one online since it was a custom build. I needed to come up with my own technique to make my own.

After some thinking and searching, I found some clear plastic sheets that I didn't need anymore. I made a frame out of leftover wood from my shop, and secured a plastic sheet within it. I then heated it in my oven for a few minutes until it was soft, and then quickly took it out and stretched it over the block I had carved.

This worked a lot better than I expected! I was able to get the exact shape I wanted. I still redid it a few times to experiment with different oven temperatures and times to try to minimize the bubbles and fogging that I was getting, and eventually I got a good one.

I cut it out of the frame and installed it on the plane, and the project was complete!

Here are some more images of the final product. I did try to fly the plane, but I crashed it during the takeoff. I was able to repair the damage, but I haven't tried to fly again since then :(. Also, after the project was complete, I made a nice walnut frame for my reference plans and hung it on the wall of my room.

Walnut Desk

Workbench

One of my first projects was building this workbench:

Working with hand tools requires a sturdy workbench to dampen vibrations and hold the piece in place, which was why I decided to build this. I made it out of sugar maple, a very dense hardwood. The top is four inches thick and the bench weighs more than 200 pounds. It also includes a custom wagon vise that I designed to easily clamp my work pieces down.

I have been using this workbench for around four years now, and it has been very helpful in making many of my other projects.
